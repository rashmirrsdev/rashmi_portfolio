7 — Day 0 of Placements: Four Interviews in One Day

Rashmi Rama Sushil · December 2022 · 7 min read

December 1st, 2022. Four companies had shortlisted me: Bajaj, JP Morgan & Chase, Morgan Stanley, and Honda. All four interviewed on the same day.

This is what happened in each one.

Bajaj — R&D (Mechanical Engineering profile)

Bajaj was looking for mechanical engineering R&D candidates, which aligned with my undergraduate background. The process was two rounds.

Round 1: Discussion about my research work — what I'd done, how I'd done it, what the outcomes were. Standard for a research-adjacent role.

HR Round: Questions about willingness to relocate, family background, and motivation for joining. The interviewers were visibly tired by the early morning session, which was something to navigate.

I was selected.

JP Morgan & Chase — Risk Analyst

JPMC had multiple elimination rounds. Each round was genuinely technical.

Round 1: Probability — Gaussian distributions, coin-toss expected values. OOP basics and binary search.

Round 2: Machine learning — XGBoost (explain how it works, when you'd use it). Data structures, tree traversals, matrix search problems.

Round 3: Regression and logistic regression — assumptions, interpretation, diagnostics. Optimisation techniques.

HR Round: Standard behavioural questions.

The rounds were sequential — you had to pass each one to continue. I made it through all rounds and received an offer.

Morgan Stanley — Strats and Quant Researcher

Three rounds. This was the role I was most nervous about because of the compressed preparation time.

Round 1: Technical problem-solving. A well-known type: cricket player selection optimisation — given constraints on roles (batsmen, bowlers, all-rounders) and a budget, select the optimal team. This is a classic combinatorial optimisation problem. I worked through it systematically.

Round 2: Probability puzzles. These included the standard types that appear in quant interviews — expected value problems, conditional probability, Bayesian reasoning. The key is staying calm and working through the logic step by step rather than trying to recall a memorised answer.

Round 3: Behavioural — questions about milestone management, handling workplace disagreements, how I approach ambiguous problems.

I received an offer.

Honda — AI Researcher (International Profile)

The Honda interview was different from the others. Rather than structured technical rounds, it was a 30–40 minute conversation with the project head about research alignment and work culture.

The conversation focused on: my research publications and how they related to Honda's interests, how I approached research problems, and whether I'd be comfortable with an international role (the team was based in Japan).

It felt more like a research discussion than an interview. My publications were directly relevant to what they were working on, which made the conversation natural.

The outcome

By the end of December 1st, I had received offers from Bajaj, JP Morgan & Chase, and Morgan Stanley. After careful consideration, I accepted Morgan Stanley.

I started in July 2023. I was promoted to Senior Associate in January 2025. That decision turned out to be the right one for me — but the right offer is different for everyone. Think about what you want to learn, what environment you want to work in, and where the role can take you in three to five years.

The preparation was worth it. It just didn't look like it would be, for a long time before it was.

If you have questions about any part of this process, find me on LinkedIn. Good luck.
