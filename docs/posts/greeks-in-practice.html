What Delta, Vega, Gamma, and Theta actually mean when you're managing a live portfolio

Rashmi Rama Sushil · March 2026 · Interactive

The Greeks appear in every quant finance course. Here's how I actually think about each one when monitoring a live derivatives book — not as formulas to calculate, but as a language for communicating risk.

Δ
Delta
Your directional exposure. How much your position gains/loses per $1 move in the underlying.
Γ
Gamma
How fast your Delta changes. High Gamma = your exposure accelerates as the market moves.
V
Vega
Your sensitivity to implied volatility. Long options = positive Vega. Vol spikes hurt option sellers.
Θ
Theta
Daily time decay. Options lose value as expiry approaches. Sellers collect it. Buyers pay it.

The fundamental trade-off: buyers vs sellers

Every options position sits on one side of this table. Which side is better depends entirely on whether realised vol exceeds implied vol.

Option Buyer (Long)Option Seller (Short)
DeltaPositive (call) / Negative (put)Opposite of buyer
Gamma+ Positive (convexity)− Negative
Vega+ Benefits from vol rise− Hurt by vol rise
Theta− Pays daily decay+ Earns daily decay
Wins when…Realised vol > Implied volRealised vol < Implied vol
In practice: You never look at one Greek in isolation. A book might look Vega-neutral in aggregate but hide concentration at specific strikes or expiries. The skill is reading them together — as a story about how the portfolio is positioned relative to what the market might do next.
