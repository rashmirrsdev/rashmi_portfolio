Placement Journey

3 — Target Companies and Preparation Timeline

Rashmi Rama Sushil · March 2023 · 8 min read

Before I get into the timeline, an honest note: this preparation started during what turned out to be the biggest tech recession the world had seen. Hiring froze at many companies. The uncertainty was real. I'm sharing this timeline not as a blueprint but as a record of what I actually did — including the parts that didn't go to plan.

Target companies and roles

I targeted companies in data science, analytics, and quantitative research:

  • Data Scientist
  • Analytics Scientist / Trading Analytics / Quantitative Analyst / Research Analyst
  • Data Engineer
  • ML Engineer
  • AI Researcher

July — Online courses

I completed the courses listed in my master resume. Andrew Ng's Introduction to Deep Learning was the main one — five modules, foundational knowledge across deep learning topics. The website suggests six months; with intensive study it's completable in 3–4 weeks. The final module on transformers is more detailed but worth it.

August — Coding started

I had no CS background. I started Python and DSA from scratch (the full account is in Post 4). The main DSA topics covered in August:

  • Arrays and Strings
  • Searching and Sorting
  • 2D Arrays
  • Dynamic Programming (basics)

By end of August I could read and write basic code on LeetCode and GFG, and I had developed curiosity about what I didn't yet understand.

September — Aptitude and continued coding

Aptitude practice started with morning timed sessions. I focused on quantitative aptitude, not logical reasoning. Key strategy: always practise with a timer. Aim to solve one question per minute — in a 25-minute session, you should be able to complete around 25 questions.

Topics: Number System, Profit & Loss, Pipes and Cisterns, Percentages, Mixtures and Alligations, Ratio and Proportion, Time and Work, Speed/Distance/Time, Probability, HCF/LCM, Simple Interest, Partnership.

Resources: Standard aptitude books and IndiaBIX for online practice.

DSA continued: Backtracking, Heaps and Hashing, Stacks and Queues, Linked Lists.

October — Technical tests begin

Tests started in October. Many companies, many formats — some with five sections, some with two, some with high-level aptitude, some with heavy coding. Some tests were offline (invigilated at a designated centre), which added travel time and fatigue to the equation.

I was taking up to three tests in a single day at points. It was exhausting.

November — Resume preparation and ML revision

By November I had only one interview shortlist. With the test-based filtering not producing results, I shifted focus to preparing for interviews — assuming I would eventually get through.

Method: I went section by section through my resume. For each item, I created a row in a spreadsheet: the topic, the technical keywords, the sub-topics to revise. Then I revised by searching questions, not by reading notes passively.

For statistics and ML:

  • StatQuest by Josh Starmer — excellent. He explains every topic in a way that covers all common interview questions. Max 20 minutes per video. I watched the full ML and stats playlists in 2–3 days and took notes in Q&A format.
  • Introduction to Statistical Learning — good foundation. Read actively: identify questions as you go, don't just read through.
  • Hands-On Machine Learning with Scikit-Learn, Keras, and TensorFlow — practical reference.

The last 10 days before placements were when the shortlists started coming in: General Electric, Bajaj, Exxon Mobil, JP Morgan & Chase. Then, just 10 hours before Day 0, Morgan Stanley shortlisted me.
