6 — A Week Before Placements: Company-Specific Prep

Rashmi Rama Sushil · April 2023 · 5 min read

The final week before Day 0 was chaotic. Shortlists came in late, with varying amounts of notice. Here's how I prepared for the three companies that ended up interviewing me — Honda, JP Morgan & Chase, and Morgan Stanley — and what I would do differently.

Honda — AI Researcher

I received the Honda shortlist with about a week's notice, which was relatively generous. I had scored in the top 15 of 900+ applicants in their technical assessment (30 min: 2 coding questions, 15 min: 10 aptitude + logical).

With a week to prepare, I built a six-point revision plan:

  1. Research presentation — prepare to discuss my publications clearly and confidently
  2. Anticipated technical questions — computer vision basics (relevant to autonomous driving research), statistics, ML fundamentals
  3. Cultural adjustment — Honda's interviewers were from Japan; I prepared to communicate clearly and simply
  4. Technical confidence — being able to speak fluently about my research methods
  5. Company research — understanding Honda's R&D focus areas
  6. Statistics review — particularly the areas relevant to their research interests

The key was that my research background was directly relevant to what Honda was looking for — autonomous driving and AI. I didn't need to fake expertise; I needed to communicate what I'd actually done clearly.

JP Morgan & Chase — Risk Analyst

The JPMC test was one of the harder ones — three sections covering mathematics, probability, and coding. For interview prep, I compiled previously asked JPMC interview questions from GFG and Glassdoor and studied variations on those questions rather than trying to cover everything.

Focus areas: probability (Gaussian distributions, expected values, coin-toss problems), basic OOP, binary search, XGBoost, tree traversals, matrix problems, regression, logistic regression, optimisation techniques.

The approach of starting from known past questions and working outward is more efficient than trying to cover topics in isolation.

Morgan Stanley — Strats and Quant Researcher

This was the most compressed. I received the Morgan Stanley shortlist less than 10 hours before Day 0. I had two hours to prepare.

I assembled a spreadsheet of past Morgan Stanley interview questions (from GFG, Glassdoor, and any other sources I could find quickly) and went through them as fast as I could. The test format covered: English (basic), quantitative reasoning (basic), logical reasoning, finance, and coding.

Finance was the section I was least prepared for — I had no finance background at that point. I revised the basics I could get through in the time available and focused on being honest about what I didn't know during the interview rather than trying to bluff.

There was also a Bajaj interview scheduled for 2am the same night, which I'd find out about just before Day 0 began.
