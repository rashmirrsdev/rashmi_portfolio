From IIT Madras research to Morgan Stanley front office: what the transition actually looked like

Rashmi Rama Sushil · June 2026 · 6 min read

When I was finishing my MS Research at IIT Madras in 2023, I knew I wanted to move into finance — specifically into something analytical, something that used the quantitative skills I'd built during my research, but applied to markets and real capital rather than academic problems.

What I didn't know was exactly what that path would look like, how to position a research background for finance roles, or what the day-to-day work of a risk or analytics role actually involved. This post is what I would have wanted to read then.

The background I came with

My MS Research was in Engineering Design, with a focus on machine learning methods for large dataset analysis and visualisation. I published four papers during my time there — in Springer journals and at international conferences in the US and Japan — and spent time teaching a class of 80+ students as a TA. The CGPA was 9.56/10, which I worked hard for, but the more valuable outcome was learning how to break down a complex problem, structure an approach, and communicate findings to different audiences.

What I didn't have was any finance background. No CFA, no financial modelling courses, no internships in the sector. I knew Python and statistics. I didn't know what Delta was.

Why trading risk management

I joined Morgan Stanley's Trading Risk Management team as an Associate in July 2023. The role appealed because it sat at the intersection of quantitative analysis and live markets — you're not just building models in isolation, you're monitoring a real derivatives book with real capital at risk, explaining P&L movements to traders and senior management, and building tools that the team depends on daily.

The onboarding was steep. The portfolio I joined covered commodity derivatives — crude oil, EU power and natural gas, LME metals, agricultural products — across hundreds of different instruments globally. I had to learn the market structure, the instrument types, the risk framework, and the reporting infrastructure simultaneously.

What made the transition work

The research skills transferred more directly than I expected. Structuring a problem, knowing which variables matter and which are noise, communicating quantitative findings clearly to non-technical stakeholders — these are the same skills whether you're writing a paper or presenting a risk briefing to an MD. The vocabulary is different, but the underlying discipline is the same.

Python was immediately useful. The team works in Python for analytics and automation, and being able to build tools from scratch — not just use existing ones — made a real difference early on. Within the first few months I was building and extending reporting tools that the team ran daily.

What I had to build from scratch was the market knowledge. That came from daily Bloomberg monitoring, from reading everything I could about commodity markets, and from paying close attention to why things moved when they moved. You develop a feel for the commodity complex through repeated observation — there's no shortcut. After three years, that intuition is one of the most valuable things I have.

What was harder than expected

The pace of a front-office environment is different from research. In research, you work on a problem until you've solved it properly — time horizons are weeks or months. In a trading environment, you need to understand why the P&L moved today, produce accurate analysis under time pressure, and communicate it clearly to people who are busy and need the point fast. That adjustment took a few months.

Finance also has more inherited complexity than academic work — legacy systems, non-obvious conventions, institutional knowledge that lives in colleagues rather than documentation. Navigating that requires patience and a lot of asking questions.

Where I am now

I was promoted to Senior Associate in January 2025. The scope of work has expanded — I now lead the P&L and risk review forums with Director and MD-level leadership, and I've built and operationalised the reporting infrastructure the team runs on. I also hold CFA Level 1, which I completed alongside work.

What's next for me is a move into a more explicitly analytical role — portfolio analytics, quantitative research, or risk modelling — where the work involves not just monitoring and reporting risk but actively modelling and interpreting it. That's the direction I'm building toward, and the projects I'm working on (see the Projects page) are part of that preparation.

If you're at a similar stage

If you have a strong quantitative background — research, engineering, applied mathematics — and you're considering a move into finance, the most important thing to know is that the analytical skills transfer. The finance knowledge is learnable. What matters is the ability to think rigorously about complex problems, communicate quantitative findings clearly, and build things that actually work.

The placement journey that led to this role is documented in the For Students section of this site — seven posts covering preparation, mindset, company-specific prep, and what Day 0 actually felt like. If you're going through the same process, that might be useful reading.
