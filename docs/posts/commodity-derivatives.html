How commodity markets are structurally different from equities

Before you can interpret what's happening in a commodity book, you need to understand why commodity markets behave the way they do. Many intuitions built on equities simply don't transfer.

📦

Physical delivery anchors the price

Equity prices can go wherever sentiment takes them. Commodity prices are constrained by physical reality — storage has cost, pipelines have capacity limits, and you cannot create more crude oil on demand. Price cannot stay arbitrarily far from physical cost for long.

📅

Seasonality is structural, not cyclical

EU natural gas prices have systematic winter premia baked into the forward curve every year — not because of macro cycles, but because cold weather drives physical demand. Agricultural prices follow planting and harvest calendars. This is physical reality, not sentiment.

🏭

Concentrated supply, fragmented demand

OPEC+ controls ~40% of global crude supply. Chile and Peru together produce ~40% of global copper. A mine strike, a weather event in the US Midwest, or a geopolitical shock can cut supply 10–20% with no easy near-term substitute — something equity markets simply don't face.

📈

The futures curve shape tells you more than spot

Steep backwardation means the physical market is tight and spot demand is high. Contango means storage is cheap and the market is oversupplied. In March 2020, crude flipped from backwardation to super-contango in weeks as storage filled. Equities have no equivalent structural signal.

Implied volatility: commodities vs equities

Commodity vol is structurally higher than equities and spikes much faster. EU power can move from 30% to 180%+ vol on a single cold snap. Agricultural vol can double in a week on a USDA crop report. Each desk has its own distinct vol regime driven by physical supply/demand factors — not just macro sentiment.

What three years actually taught me

Lesson 01

The market doesn't care about your model

Specific assumptions break — sometimes overnight. Mean reversion: commodity prices can stay far from historical averages for months during structural supply shocks (EU gas in 2022 is the textbook case). Correlation: Brent-WTI spreads that held tight for years can decouple in hours on a pipeline disruption. Seasonality: La Niña breaks agricultural seasonal patterns that were consistent for a decade. Term structure: a book in backwardation for months can flip to super-contango in a week as physical storage fills up. The model is a starting point, not an answer — and the market reminds you of that regularly.

Lesson 02

Commodities are driven by physical markets

There's no risk-free rate equivalent for physical commodities. Storage costs, transport, and supply chain bottlenecks are in the price. Understanding why a position moves requires understanding the underlying physical market, not just the instrument.

Lesson 03

Greeks are a language, not a number

When explaining to a trader why P&L dropped 2% on a flat spot day, you're not reporting Delta — you're telling a story about how vol moved, how gamma worked near expiry, how the curve shifted. Getting fluent takes daily repetition.

Lesson 04

P&L attribution is harder than it looks

The number at end of day is just the output. Delta P&L, Vega P&L, Gamma P&L, Theta — breaking these apart and reconciling them to the actual number requires precision. Unexplained residuals are where the most interesting risk lives.

The one thing I'd tell someone starting: Read the market narrative every morning before you look at the positions. Know why prices moved before you try to explain why your P&L moved. The attribution follows from understanding the market — not the other way around.
