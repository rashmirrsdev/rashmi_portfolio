When I joined Morgan Stanley's trading risk team in mid-2023, I had a strong quantitative background from IIT Madras but had never seen a live derivatives book up close. Three years later, I monitor one every day. Here's what that experience actually taught me.

One of the most diverse commodity derivatives books in the firm — spanning four asset classes across global markets:

Typical implied volatility ranges by commodity class

Commodity vol is structurally higher than equities — and spikes much faster. Each class has its own vol regime driven by different physical market factors.

What three years actually taught me

Lesson 01

The market doesn't care about your model

Clean assumptions hold right until they don't. EU power spikes 40% overnight on a cold snap. LME copper gaps down on macro news at 3am. You learn to separate what the model says from what the market is doing — and why they diverge.

Lesson 02

Commodities are driven by physical markets

There's no risk-free rate equivalent for physical commodities. Storage costs, transport, and supply chain bottlenecks are in the price. Understanding why a position moves requires understanding the underlying physical market, not just the instrument.

Lesson 03

Greeks are a language, not a number

When explaining to a trader why P&L dropped 2% on a flat spot day, you're not reporting Delta — you're telling a story about how vol moved, how gamma worked near expiry, how the curve shifted. Getting fluent takes daily repetition.

Lesson 04

P&L attribution is harder than it looks

The number at end of day is just the output. Delta P&L, Vega P&L, Gamma P&L, Theta — breaking these apart and reconciling them to the actual number requires precision. Unexplained residuals are where the most interesting risk lives.

The one thing I'd tell someone starting: Read the market narrative every morning before you look at the positions. Know why prices moved before you try to explain why your P&L moved. The attribution follows from understanding the market — not the other way around.
