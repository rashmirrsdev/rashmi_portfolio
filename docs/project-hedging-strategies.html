Hedging a stock position: six strategies and what each one actually costs you

A payoff visualiser for collars, protective puts, spreads, and straddles — built to make the trade-offs between protection and participation visible in rupee terms.

View code on GitHub Completed · June 2026

The question hedging is really asking

When someone says they've "hedged" a position, that statement is almost always incomplete. What did they give up to hedge it? How much does the protection cost — not just in premium, but in capped upside, in shifted breakevens, in complexity? Every hedge is a trade-off, and the payoff diagram is the clearest way to see exactly what that trade-off is.

I built this project around a concrete scenario: you hold 100 shares of RELIANCE at INR 2,800. The market is uncertain. You want to reduce downside risk — but the question is which approach you take, because each one gives you a different P&L profile. The chart below shows all six strategies at once, measured at expiry.

All six strategies vs the unhedged position (grey dashed). Each strategy has a distinct shape: some floor the downside, some cap the upside, some profit from large moves in either direction.

The zero-cost collar: not as free as it sounds

The zero-cost collar is often presented as the best of both worlds: you buy a put for downside protection and sell a call to finance it, paying no net premium. In our scenario, the INR 2,600 put costs INR 48/share; the INR 3,000 call is sold for INR 52/share — a small net credit. No cash out of pocket.

But look at what you've traded away. The left side of the collar: your loss is floored at approximately INR 19,600 — you can't lose more than that no matter how far RELIANCE falls. The right side: your gain is capped at INR 20,400 — if RELIANCE goes to 3,500, you miss that entire move. The collar cost nothing in premium but it cost you your participation in a large rally.

Left: the three collar components combining — long stock (grey), long put protection (blue dashed), short call cap (red dashed), and the resulting collar (green). Right: the collar profile alone, with the put and call strikes marked. The flat regions above and below show exactly where the cap and floor kick in.

The protective put: insurance has a cost basis

A protective put (long stock + long put) is the simplest hedge: you pay INR 48/share for the right to sell at INR 2,600 regardless of where RELIANCE goes. Your downside is floored. Your upside is unlimited.

What's not immediately obvious: the premium you paid shifts your breakeven. Without the hedge you break even at INR 2,800. With the put, you break even at INR 2,848 — RELIANCE needs to rise INR 48 just to cover the insurance cost. If RELIANCE stays flat, you lose the premium. If it rises modestly, you've paid for protection you didn't need.

This is the core tension of protective puts: the scenarios where you most want the insurance are the ones where you're glad you have it, but in every other scenario you've paid unnecessarily. The collar eliminates that cost — at the price of capping your upside.

The protection spectrum: unhedged (grey dashed), protective put (blue), and zero-cost collar (green). Below the put strike, both hedges floor the loss. Above the call strike, the collar caps while the protective put keeps rising. The vertical gap between blue and green in the upper range is the cost of choosing zero-premium over full participation.

Spreads: buying the direction, selling the expensive part

A bull call spread is a cheaper way to express a bullish view. Instead of buying a single call (expensive), you buy the lower strike call and sell the higher strike call to offset some of the cost. In our example: long the INR 2,800 call, short the INR 3,000 call. Net debit: INR 52/share (vs. perhaps INR 98/share for just the outright call).

What you've sold away: the profitable part of the move above INR 3,000. If RELIANCE goes to 3,400, the spread still only pays you the difference between the two strikes minus the premium — INR 14,800 total. The pure call would have paid much more. But the spread cost less than half as much to enter, and the maximum loss is just INR 5,200 vs. INR 9,800 for the outright call.

Left: bull call spread (purple) vs bear put spread (red) — mirrored payoff profiles, one bullish, one bearish, both with defined risk and capped reward. Right: long straddle (orange) — profits when RELIANCE moves more than INR 189 in either direction. The flat bottom of the straddle is the maximum premium cost (INR 18,900), incurred when the stock goes nowhere.

The straddle: a bet on movement, not direction

The straddle (long ATM call + long ATM put) is structurally different from all the other strategies. It doesn't care which way RELIANCE moves — it profits from a large move in either direction. What it needs is movement.

The breakeven points sit at INR 2,611 and INR 2,989 — RELIANCE needs to move at least INR 189 (6.75%) in either direction by expiry for the straddle to be profitable. Inside that range, the position loses money every day from time decay. At expiry with RELIANCE exactly at INR 2,800, you lose the full INR 18,900 in premium.

Straddles are typically bought before a major event — a results announcement, a regulatory decision, a macro data release — where the direction is unclear but a large move is expected. The trade-off is that you pay for two option premiums, and time is working against you from the moment you enter.

What building this showed me

Working through the exact numbers — not qualitative descriptions but actual INR payoffs at every spot price — made the trade-offs much more concrete. The collar's "zero cost" framing always bothered me slightly because it obscures the real cost, which is the forfeited upside. The payoff diagram makes that visible immediately: the two flat regions on either side are the exact amount you've given up.

The straddle breakeven calculation (INR 189, or 6.75% in either direction) is another one that's obvious in a diagram but easy to underestimate when you think about it abstractly. Before a results announcement, you're essentially betting that the stock moves more than implied vol suggests it will. If the market has already priced a 6% move and the actual move is 5%, you lose — even if your directional call was right.
